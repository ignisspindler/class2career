Workbook 5: Personal Growth

Class2Career: Becoming Your Best Self in Business and Life
By Ignis Spindler

Introduction: This Is About You

The first four workbooks were about business. This one is about you.

Your career isn’t just a series of jobs. It’s a journey — and you’re the one driving.

Your instructor: “You should really be flexible and imagine your dream. The dream of what you can be making, the best money you can do while doing the thing you love and what it takes to get there, to be recognized for a thing that is you.”

This workbook isn’t about learning business vocabulary or frameworks. It’s about understanding yourself — your ambitions, your tolerances, your values, and your purpose.

Let’s get honest.

Part 1: Vision — Defining Your Destination

The First Question

Before you can get where you’re going, you need to know where you want to end up.

Your instructor: “Have you seriously triaged your ambitions? That’s my first question for you.”

Ambition is tricky. We all believe we have it. But are you willing to do the work it takes to get there?

Self-Assessment: Your Current State

Before you plan your future, honestly evaluate where you are now:

Your Ambitions - What do you want your career to look like in 5 years? 10 years? - What kind of work do you want to be doing? - What lifestyle do you want to support?

Your Work Ethic - How many hours per week are you willing to work? - Are you willing to make sacrifices now for future gains? - What’s your tolerance for discomfort?

Your Risk Tolerance - Are you okay with uncertainty? - Would you join a startup with potential but no guarantee? - Can you handle financial instability?

Your instructor: “Risk is very much a part of this because it is a risk to go work for a small business… But risk tolerance can also come with reward, major reward.”

Part 2: Understanding Your Tolerances

The Questions Nobody Asks

When’s the last time someone asked you:

  • What’s your tolerance for people problems?
  • What’s your tolerance for technical frustration?
  • What’s your tolerance for criticism?
  • What’s your tolerance for failure?

These matter more than you think.

People Problems

Your instructor: “Everybody has people problems. Everybody has social issues here and there. Somebody they clash with, they don’t like working with.”

Ask yourself: - Do you like being around people or do you prefer working alone? - How do you handle conflict? - Can you work with difficult personalities?

Technical Work

“Slaving away at code and dealing with really technical problems with computers can eventually just get on your nerves.”

Some people thrive on deep technical work. Others burn out. Know which one you are.

Criticism and Failure

“If you get into management or you start working your way up a corporate ladder, you become a target for criticism.”

  • Can you receive feedback without taking it personally?
  • Can you deliver bad news to stakeholders?
  • Can you handle public failure?

Your instructor: “In academia, you get enough help and you’re given enough time that you’re kind of always working towards a goal of success, but you never really deeply experience quote-unquote failure.”

Business is different. Failure is visible. It’s tangible. It affects the bottom line.

Part 3: Getting Resourceful — The Leverage Mindset

You Are the CEO of Your Career

Your instructor: “Think about your career like you are the entrepreneur of it. You define it. You can turn it into gold no matter where it’s at right now.”

This is the most important mindset shift.

You’re not waiting for opportunities to find you. You’re hunting for them.

The Concept of Leverage

Tim Ferriss (author of The 4-Hour Workweek) says:

“Every day you should spend three full hours on the highest leverage thing in your to-do list.”

Leverage = Getting more out than you put in.

Ways to create leverage: - Information — Knowledge is power. The more you know, the more valuable you are. - Technology — Tools that multiply your output. - Assets — Resources that work for you. - People — Delegation and collaboration.

Multiply Your Strengths

Your instructor: “Multiplying your strengths is a way of not wasting time trying to improve your mediocrities. It takes a long time to improve your mediocrities. But if you’re already good at four different things and you don’t think they’re related, figure out some way that you can bring those four things together.”

Your unique combination of skills is your niche. Nobody else has your exact blend.

Part 4: The Tensions Within You

The Academic vs. Business Tension

Your instructor identifies key tensions that shape your career:

Academic Tendency Business Reality
Perfectionism Pragmatism
Deep specialization Generalization
Research mode Action mode
Independence Collaboration

Perfectionism vs. Pragmatism: “You need to redefine what done is, because perfectionism stops you from being done. And the business really needs you to be done.”

Generalization vs. Customization: “Businesses with general solutions are typically able to grow bigger. Customization can really lock you in a box.”

Self-Directed vs. Managed Work

“When you go towards a business that is very small, you really have to go after the problems that appear to you… That’s very self-directed. Is it better to be told what to do? I definitely think it’s easier.”

Some people thrive with autonomy. Others need structure. Neither is wrong — but you need to know which one you are.

Recognition vs. Accountability

“I prefer to go the accountable route. I really don’t want recognition.”

What about you? Do you want to be seen, or do you just want to do the work?

Part 5: The Work on Yourself

The Uncomfortable Questions

Your instructor: “Are you willing to ask other people what they think of you?”

This is tough. But it’s how you grow.

Social Savviness

“How much work are you willing to do to become a 10 at social savviness?”

Not everyone needs to be the “life of the party.” But if you want to advance in business, social skills matter.

Your instructor: “It’s a big deal in sales to be social. It’s a big deal in leadership.”

Public Speaking

“Can you imagine having to present to a room full of people?”

Your instructor: “Teaching in a public school taught me more about public speaking… If you can entertain 14 to 18-year-olds for seven hours a day, that’s a great start. Adults are much easier.”

Leadership Challenges

Can you imagine: - Firing someone? - Laying off a team? - Announcing bad results to investors?

These are the hard parts of leadership. Not everyone is built for them — and that’s okay.

Part 6: Building Your Personal Board of Directors

You Need Advisors

Your instructor: “Executives always have personal development sessions. They spend so much time doing it because they really do feel that these things change their ability to interact with people.”

You don’t have to figure this out alone.

Find Your People

  • Mentors — People who’ve been where you want to go
  • Coaches — Professionals who help you see blind spots
  • Peers — People at similar stages who can relate
  • Sponsors — People who advocate for you when you’re not in the room

Resources to Leverage

Your instructor: “I am telling you, regardless of your approach… think about your career like you are the entrepreneur of it.”

Use: - Career services at your school - Professional associations - Online communities - Books and podcasts - Meetup groups

Part 7: Purpose — The Long Game

What Drives You?

Your instructor closes with something deeper:

“There’s a belief system that everybody has… When I try to adapt these things to business, I saw the business itself, all the pieces of the business, and the fact that it’s like an organism, literally it is like an organism.”

Your career isn’t just about money. It’s about meaning.

The Four Elements Framework

Your instructor draws from ancient philosophy:

  • Earth — The body. Your health, your work, your practical life.
  • Water — Your emotions. How you handle momentum, energy, relationships.
  • Air — Your mind. Your thinking, learning, creativity.
  • Fire — Your spirit. Your purpose, your drive, your “why.”

Balance all four. Ignore any one, and something breaks.

Legacy

What do you want to leave behind?

Your instructor: “Why can’t employment be a holistic community of sacred values?”

Your work can be more than a paycheck. It can be a contribution.

Workbook Exercises

Exercise 1: The Vision Exercise

Write out your answers to these questions: 1. Where do I want to be in 5 years? 2. What does success look like to me? 3. What am I willing to sacrifice to get there? 4. What am I NOT willing to sacrifice?

Exercise 2: The Tolerance Audit

Rate yourself 1-10 on each: - Tolerance for people problems: - Tolerance for technical work: - Tolerance for criticism: - Tolerance for failure:

Be honest. This isn’t about what you should feel. It’s about what you actually feel.

Exercise 3: The Leverage Inventory

List: 1. Your top 3 strengths 2. How you can multiply them 3. One way to create leverage this month

Exercise 4: The Feedback Ask

Identify 3 people who know your work. Ask them: - “What’s one thing I should start doing?” - “What’s one thing I should stop doing?” - “What’s one thing I should continue doing?”

Listen. Don’t defend.

Exercise 5: The Purpose Reflection

Complete these sentences: - I feel most alive when… - I want to be remembered for… - My work matters because… - One thing I want to contribute to the world is…

Exercise 6: The 10-Year Letter

Write a letter from your future self (10 years from now) to your present self. What do they wish you’d known? What do they wish you’d done?

Book Recommendations

  1. The 4-Hour Workweek by Tim Ferriss — Escape the 9-5, leverage your time, design your lifestyle.

  2. Drive by Daniel Pink — What actually motivates us: autonomy, mastery, and purpose.

  3. Grit by Angela Duckworth — The power of passion and perseverance.

  4. Mindset by Carol Dweck — Growth mindset vs. fixed mindset.

  5. Emotional Intelligence 2.0 by Travis Bradberry — Practical EQ skills.

  6. The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People by Stephen Covey — Classic personal development.

  7. Reinventing the Sacred by Stuart Kauffman — Complexity, meaning, and purpose. (Your instructor’s recommendation.)

Reflection Questions

  1. What’s one tolerance you need to accept about yourself?

  2. Where in your life do you need more leverage?

  3. What’s the gap between where you are and where you want to be?

  4. What would your 80-year-old self thank you for doing today?

  5. How do you want your work to matter?

A Final Word

Your instructor: “Your career is yours. You define it.”

This isn’t about becoming someone else. It’s about becoming more you — the best version of yourself in the context of the career you want.

Do the work. Ask the hard questions. Get uncomfortable.

And remember: you are the entrepreneur of your career.

Now go build something.
